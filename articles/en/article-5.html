Bangladesh's Political Turmoil: From St. Martin's to the Streets

When Local Incompetence Outsourced Sovereignty

Published: January 7, 2026

By: KVR

South Asian Politics | Strategic Affairs

St. Martin's Island is the opening scene: its salience legally reinforced by the 2012 ITLOS ruling and haunted by Cold War rumours from the 1960s–1980s; a 2018 Myanmar map error briefly reignited anxieties. By 2023, China's BRI moves in Myanmar and the commissioning of the BNS Sheikh Hasina submarine base near Cox's Bazar in 2023 sharpened great‑power focus. When Myanmar's civil war began spilling over from February 2024, St. Martin's value exploded. Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina alleged in 2023–2024 that foreign actors dangled "hassle free" elections for strategic access—claims Washington denied—turning maritime paranoia into combustible political fuel.

Cut to 2023: St. Martin's sits astride sea-lanes to the Strait of Malacca—roughly 80% of China's energy imports transit there. Beijing is deepening ties (the BNS Sheikh Hasina base in 2023; Kyaukpyu port under the BRI) transformed a regional flashpoint into a strategic problem. The United States then internationalised the issue, pitching the island as a potential surveillance, intelligence or military/air hub to monitor and counter China across the Bay of Bengal and the Indo‑Pacific.

The Quota Fault Line and the Legal Cascade

The proximate fault was bureaucratic: for decades 30% of civil‑service posts were reserved for descendants of 1971 "freedom fighters," and layered quotas for women, districts, minorities and the disabled pushed total reservations past 56%, targeting a vote bank and leaving under half of posts open to merit. The High Court's insistence that quota slots remain vacant created thousands of unusable posts. In 2018, the government abolished quotas by executive circular, producing paralysis; ministries froze and courts probed. Smouldering campus anger exploded into mass mobilisation in June–July 2024. In a landmark December 2024 High Court ruling, the Court struck down parts of the 15th Amendment and restored the 13th Amendment's caretaker provisions, reviving the pre‑amendment mechanism for non‑partisan transition governments.

Under the original caretaker law, the recently retired Chief Justice was the automatic first choice for Chief Adviser; if unavailable, the post passed to the next most senior retired judge. The 15th Amendment had removed that judicial priority, instead allowing any "qualified citizen" not affiliated with a political party to be appointed after consultations with major parties – a change that dramatically widened the shortlist and raised the political stakes.

Student groups with NGOs influence, quickly rallied behind Muhammad Yunus as the citizen caretaker – a recommendation that ignited fierce debate and, as events unfolded, reshaped the transition.

Judicial Reversal, OSF Meetings and Rapid Reforms

After the caretaker revival, high profile meetings with foreign philanthropies and governance networks followed. An OSF delegation's discussions with interim leaders in 2025—on economic rebuilding, asset tracing and combating misinformation—helped shape a rapid reform agenda. Presented as technical assistance, these interventions were domestically read as external influence shaping Dhaka's transition. Judicial reversals, OSF influenced reforms, and hurried policy changes created the perception that courts and foreign actors were jointly remaking Bangladesh's political order—fuel for those who argued the state had been hollowed out.

Student Clashes and the Mobilisation Machine

Student politics, long a proxy for national parties, ignited the streets. The Awami League's BCL clashed with JeI aligned student networks and emergent groups such as the National Citizen Party (NCP). By late 2024, media and investigators reported coordinated funding: allegations that Islamist networks and foreign donors channelled money through NGOs and student fronts to sustain logistics, transport and digital campaigns. Whether fully proven or not, the pattern suggested mobilisation beyond spontaneous protest and exposed how opaque financing can weaponise youth anger.

Two scandals crystallised grievance and fuel. Dr. Khalilur Rahman, the National Security Adviser, became a lightning rod: his long US residence, perceived foreign leanings, BNP accusations of handing over corridors and targeting rivals, alleged personal misconduct, and reports that he was barred from military installations intensified perceptions of civil military friction. And the unresolved legacy of the 2004 grenade attack—Tarique Rahman's exile to London in 2008 and the later quashing of convictions after 5 August 2024—fed narratives of victimhood and vindication that opposition networks used to recruit and radicalise.

Foreign Interference and the Dangerous Opening

Pakistan re‑entered Dhaka's orbit: Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar's August 2025 visit signalled renewed outreach; Dar rejected calls for a fresh apology and downplayed unresolved "1971 Apology". His softening toward JI reportedly signalled to figures like Rahman that full accountability wasn't required, helping explain JI's vague, tactical apology. Saudi and Libyan deals lit the fire, nudging Dhaka toward an "Islamic NATO" axis. After bans on JI‑linked outfits were eased, communal tensions flared and student‑party clashes intensified.

Asim Munir's Gambit: A military entrepreneur saw chaos as a ladder

Retired generals and security entrepreneurs saw Dhaka's wobble as a business plan. Asim Munir, billed as a bold strategist, treated institutional weakness like a ladder: Pakistan's diplomatic re‑entry was the launch, irredentist rhetoric—"Seven Sisters," Indian encirclement—was the marketing. The transaction was simple: stoke instability, then sell the cure—port access, corridor rights, training contracts—tied to a Pakistan‑anchored security architecture funded by Gulf cheques. Saudi and Libyan deals didn't just sweeten the pot; they lit the fire, pushing Dhaka toward an "Islamic NATO" pitch. Tactically, easing bans on JI‑led parties flooded the field with polarising actors so external pacts could be paraded as the only stabiliser.

When titans quarrel, the small suffer most

In the clash of titans (US and China), the western (US) giant wrenched a branch, unintentionally reducing the squirrel's (Bangladesh) home to dust. The lesson for smaller neighbours is clear: vulnerability is inevitable when living in the shadow of colliding giants.

Stabilising Dhaka requires immediate, transparent caretaker implementation under Article 58C, rigorous oversight of political and NGO financing, and multilateral confidence building around maritime security—before St. Martin's becomes someone else's foothold.
